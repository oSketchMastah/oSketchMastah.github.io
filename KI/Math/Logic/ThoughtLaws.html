Laws of Thought

The goal here is to distill a sense of the nature of mathematical reasoning through a direct analysis of the reasoning process. Through this analysis, much will be covered. You can expect to know the following by the end:

  • Construction of Truth-value Statements and their Semiotics
  • Understanding usage of relationship between Semantics and Syntax for proof and construction (explored further in other chapters)
  • Many principles for the pursuit of further knowledge

I find it is helpful to follow the path of knowledge taken by our ancestors in our own intellectual persuits, so I'll start off with the "Laws of Thought" that have been established since the times of Aristotle. There were three laws that were taken as presumptions, or things that must hold, in the structure of any valid argument. These 3 laws were as follows:

  • Law of Non-Contradiction: This law states that no valid argument can imply a contradiction, a contradiction occurs when a statement is implied to be both true and false. Put simply, it cannot be necessary for any statement to be both true and false based on ones axioms
  • Law of the Excluded Middle: This law states that any statement must be either true or false, there is no in-between. However, this was a law derived by observing straight-forward arguments, and since then we have found this isn't always true, as a statement may be indeterminate given what we know. However this can generally be proven or disproven in any particular case, and given determinacy, it seems this law then always holds and can thus be used in the deductive process.
  • Law of Identity: This law states that any thing or property must be identical to itself, in other words, any valid argument should not suggest that something is not something else which is not perceptually identical to itself in the context that it is uttered.

There are some understandings of the terms I have used in my description of these laws that will come in handy:
Ones Axioms are all of the statements that are assumed, deemed, or imposed to be true for the purpose of deducing further information. Mathematicians take axioms very seriously, and for good reason. The best Arguments are in the form of an implication or series of implications. For example, if someone asks, "Why is A the same as C":
the worst response - no reasoning given: "Because, A the same as C"
a (variably) better response: "Because, A the same as B, and B the same as C, which means that A the same as C"
This form of explanation works best when ones axioms make it clear why A is identical to B, and why it to C. It also assumes the understanding that equivalence is a transitive relation.
This applies to understanding the same way it applies to arguing or explaining. If you start off by understanding and refining your axioms, and can be sure of their applicability and truth, you can use these with an understanding of logic to derive facts that are (must be) true based on the truth of your axioms. You can then use logic with these newly derived truths to deduce further truth. By practicing this, you will enhance your sense of causal structure, and be better able to critically and objectively analyze the world around you.

It is good, initially, to understand logic as a collection of information that serves as a tool for rationally deriving factual statements based on groups of other statements that are believed to be factual. In boolean logic, the description of a statements truth (called its truth-value), can take on one of two values, true or false. Using the two-valued description of truth-value that is used in boolean logic is useful when dealing with statements that are causally related. When some degree of stochasticity is involved, a more suitable description might be that of Fuzzy logic, which uses the ideas of fuzzification of precise measures (for example, summarizing a numerical temperature with a term like "warm", which is a fuzzy term), and defuzzification of fuzzy measures (taking a fuzzy term, and coming up with an approximate numerical measure)

Logical Operators

Semiotics and Structure between Statements

In Mathematics, the use of symbols instead of words to communicate often complex statements is just one behavior adopted as a means of increasing productivity. Roughly speaking, if you replace an 8 letter word with a single symbol (meaning the symbol carries the same meaning as the word), you've reduced the time it takes to communicate the same idea by a factor of 8. When you realize that you only need a few unique words to describe what's going on in a situation, and you replace each of those words with a well-chosen symbol, the ease with which one can communicate their ideas on the situation improves drastically.

Pure Logic is no different. We allocate meaning to symbols and from that point on express our ideas in those symbols where applicable. When I communicate ideas about relationships between arbitrary statements, I'll give each unique statement its own unique symbol, and instead of trying to refer to the hypothetical statement by word, I'd refer to it using the symbol that I have made its alias. We'll also see that many statements are made by composing other statements, and these various compositions will also be referenced using a symbol, instead of a word descriptor.

** Hypothetical/ arbitrary statements are conventionally aliased using symbols such as j or f

Structure between statements comes in the form of how we can compose them to make new statements, and the relationships we can figure out between the truth-values of statements that possess different forms.

There are several compositional notions: conjunction, disjunction, implication, equivalence, and many others. You even get some structure when you consider that for every statement, there is a statement that says the exact opposite of what the first one says (if the first one claims truth, the second claims falsehood). These are collectively called logical operators, because you can think of them as "operating" on some statements to produce a new statement with a new truth-value. The logical operators are best defined by a truth-table which shows how the operator maps truth-values of the composed statements to the truth-value of the resulting statement. However, there is an intuitive way of thinking about its operation that I include with this table.

DefN : Negation (\(\neg\)): Yields statement claiming falsehood (antithesis) of other statement, replaces the word "not".

In other words, if \( R = \neg \phi \), the truth-table definition would be:
\(\phi\) \(R\)
True False
False True

DefN : Conjunction (\(\wedge\)): Yields statement claiming truth of each of two statements, replaces the word "and".

In other words, if \( R = \phi \wedge \psi \), the truth-table definition would be:
\(\phi\) \(\psi\) \(R\)
True True True
True False False
False True False
False False False

DefN : Disjunction (\(\vee\)): Yields statement claiming truth of atleast one of two statements, replaces the word "or".

In other words, if \( R = \phi \vee \psi \), the truth-table definition would be:
\(\phi\) \(\psi\) \(R\)
True True True
True False True
False True True
False False False

DefN : Implication (\(\implies\)): Yields statement claiming that truth of first statement implies truth of second, replaces the word "implies".

In other words, if \( R = (\phi \implies \psi) \), the truth-table definition would be:
\(\phi\) \(\psi\) \(R\)
True True True
True False False
False True True
False False True

It is important to understand why we define the truth-table for implication this way. First of all, all results must either be true or false, so we have to choose. Next, We must realize that in determining if the truth of \(\phi\) implies the truth of \(\psi\), we are showing that when \(\phi\) is true, that \(\psi\) is then also true. We don't care about when \(\phi\) is false, because then \(\psi\) could be anything and its truth-value status would not affect the truth-value of the overall statement. The rule is that such a statement is false if and only if \(\phi\) is true but \(\psi\) is nevertheless false.

DefN : Material Equivalence (\(\iff\)): Yields statement claiming two statements to have the same truth-value, replaces phrases "if and only if"

In other words, if \( R = (\phi \iff \psi) \), the truth-table definition would be:
\(\phi\) \(\psi\) \(R\)
True True True
True False False
False True False
False False True

These are all the logical operators that I care to define, although there are others that some care to use such as exclusive disjunction (aliased using \(\oplus\)) or reverse implication (a backwards \(\implies\)). These aren't as fundamental, and in fact, any statement involving any other logical operators can be rewritten (be shown equivalent) to a statement using just negations, conjunctions, and disjunctions. It's worth defining the ones I have because equivalence and implication constitute a rather significant part of our discourse in this chapter, and mathematics as a whole, and thus I care to address them directly.

Before I move on entirely, it's worth mentioning how to translate statements uttered by humans into these semiotically prescribed "forms" shown above.
Use the statements semantics, which is constant between the semiotic and human spoken versions of the statement. For example, semantically, perhaps you can tell that some uttered statement was an implication, then, you can semantically seperate it into a hypothesis (something which is assumed to be true), and a consequence (proposed to be true as a consequence of the hypothesis). If you can do that, you've already put the spoken version of the implication into the same form of implication as defined above (hypothesis \(\implies\) consequence). This technique of semantic partitioning is often a handy trick.

Logical Equivalences (\(\equiv\))

I'll start off by saying that logical equivalence is a whole lot more powerful to establish than material equivalence. In mathematics, the two types of equivalences come pretty well as a package, and so it is not uncommon that students go through it without ever making the distinction. A material equivalence is a logical operator that claims truth-value equality. Whereas logical equivalence asserts truth-value equality by claiming semantic equivalence (true by pure logic and meaning). Logical equivalence is established by showing that two forms of statement have the same truth-value regardless of any content in the statement.

There are many really great, meaningful logical equivalences that you should incorporate into how you reason about everyday dilemmas. These are often directly the small change in perspective that makes the truth of a fact significantly more obvious. In fact, it's not uncommon that material equivalence is defined using the logical equivalence:

\[ (\phi \iff \psi) \equiv (\phi \implies \psi) \wedge (\psi \implies \phi)\] This definition is chosen because it's a great definition, not only can we show it equivalent to the truth-table definition, but it shows you can prove material equivalence of two statements by simply proving that both statements are implied by eachother, which is often much easier.
